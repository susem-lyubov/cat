History


Humans begun to domesticate cats around 3,600 years ago in Egypt. This was due to cats being a natural predator of rodents, which were known to plague farms. This benefitted humans, as the cats would hunt the rodents that attempted to eat up stored grain, but also benefitted the cats, as the grain would attract a constant source of food. Unlike the domestication of other animals, cats evolved alongside humans gradually, and were not bred for specific traits until very recently. Cats also rode on ships to help keep the grain safe from any rodents which attempted to illegaly stow away aboard the ship. In the modern day, the domesticated cat has many capabilities that it has gained through evolution, such as having nine lives, and a look-down shoot-down radar array in their nose section for ease of tracking prey.


Traits And Specifications


The modern domestic feline has evolved many tools and clever solutions to survive and even thrive. One of the more widely known of these tools is the electrical sound system that powers what we humans call 'purring'. This electrical system is powered by a perpetual motion machine that still confuses the best human researchers, and activates when the cat is happy. This serves to strenghten the bond with the human that the cat is interacting with, and assist the cat in gaining favors from the human. Another commonly known trait is the fact that cats have nine lives. Cats are known to be able to survive things that any other organism simply could not. The way in which cats are able to come back to life in perfect order is still unknown.

For hunting, cats have many useful tools as well. They use the AN/PVS-7 single tube binocular night vision device to see in the dark, which on top of having a silent running engine and paws that make minimal noise, help the cat to become an expert at hunting everything from rodents to things that they knock off the table. The most advanced part of a feline's hunting ability is the way in which they track prey. Most modern domesticated cats use a reduced-size version of the Westinghouse AN/AWG-10 pulse-doppler radar to track prey. This gives them an unprecedented advantage when hunting, as the radar utilizes the pulse-doppler effect to mitigate radar return from terrain, allowing them to effectively hunt small prey attempting to hide, and track the prey continuously until they are able to catch it.
